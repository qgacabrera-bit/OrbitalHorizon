Orbital Horizon

The Limit to Vision.

Orbital Horizon is a project that uses machine learning to identify exoplanets from large NASA datasets. Our goal is to make this data more accessible and help uncover new planets hidden within the data from missions like Kepler and TESS. To take part in discovering these worlds with us, you can try the simulator here.

About

Exoplanetary identification is becoming an increasingly popular area of astronomical exploration. Several survey missions have been launched with the primary objective of identifying exoplanets. Kepler is one of the more well-known transit-method satellites, and provided data for nearly a decade. Kepler was followed by its successor mission, K2, which utilized the same hardware and transit method, but maintained a different path for surveying. During both of these missions, much of the work to identify exoplanets was done manually by astrophysicists at NASA and research institutions that sponsored the missions. After the retirement of Kepler, the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), which has a similar mission of exoplanetary surveying, launched and has been collecting data since 2018.

For each of these missions (Kepler, K2, and TESS), publicly available datasets exist that include data for all confirmed exoplanets, planetary candidates, and false positives obtained by the mission (see Resources tab). For each data point, these spreadsheets also include variables such as the orbital period, transit duration, planetary radius, and much more. As this data has become public, many individuals have researched methods to automatically identify exoplanets using machine learning. But despite the availability of new technology and previous research in automated classification of exoplanetary data, much of this exoplanetary transit data is still analyzed manually. Promising research studies have shown great results can be achieved when data is automatically analyzed to identify exoplanets. Much of the research has proven that preprocessing of data, as well as the choice of model, can result in high-accuracy identification. Utilizing the Kepler, K2, TESS, and other NASA-created, open-source datasets can help lead to discoveries of new exoplanets hiding in the data these satellites have provided.

Methodology

1. Data Acquisition

We begin by sourcing curated datasets from NASA's public archives, specifically from missions like Kepler, K2, and TESS. These datasets contain thousands of light curves, which are measurements of star brightness over time.

2. Initial Preprocessing

The raw light curve data is often noisy. Our first preprocessing phase involves cleaning the data by removing outliers, normalizing the flux values, and handling any missing data points to ensure a consistent baseline for analysis.

3. Feature Engineering

From the cleaned light curves, we extract key features that are indicative of a planetary transit. This includes metrics like transit depth, duration, orbital period, and the shape of the transit signal.

4. Dimensionality Reduction

To improve model performance and reduce computational cost, we apply dimensionality reduction techniques. This helps in identifying the most impactful features and reducing noise from less important ones.

5. Model Selection

We evaluate several machine learning models suitable for classification tasks, such as XGBoost and LightGBM. The choice is based on their proven effectiveness in handling tabular data and their ability to capture complex patterns.

6. Training & Validation

The selected model is trained on a labeled dataset containing confirmed exoplanets, planetary candidates, and known false positives. We use cross-validation to ensure the model generalizes well to new, unseen data.

7. Hyperparameter Tuning

To maximize accuracy, we perform hyperparameter tuning. This process systematically searches for the optimal model settings, such as learning rate and tree depth, to achieve the best predictive performance.

8. Prediction on New Data

Once trained and tuned, the model is used to make predictions on new, unclassified light curves from the datasets. It assigns a probability score indicating the likelihood that a signal is a true exoplanet.

9. Candidate Verification

The model's high-confidence predictions are flagged as new planetary candidates. While our model provides strong evidence, these candidates would typically undergo further review by astronomers for official confirmation.

10. Visualization & Reporting

The results, including the newly identified candidates, are made available through our interactive tools. The AI Workbench allows users to run this process, and the Simulator helps visualize the potential characteristics of these new worlds.
